Azospirillum: The Cereal Root Champion

Root-Colonizing N-Fixer for Grasses & Cereals - The Monocot Specialist!

üß™ What is Azospirillum?

Azospirillum (mainly Azospirillum brasilense and A. lipoferum) is a free-living nitrogen-fixing bacterium classified as PGPR (Plant Growth Promoting Rhizobacteria). Unlike Azotobacter (which lives freely in soil) or Rhizobium (which forms nodules), Azospirillum forms an associative symbiosis with plant roots, colonizing the rhizosphere (root zone) and rhizoplane (root surface).

Azospirillum is the cereal and grass specialist - it has special affinity for monocots with fibrous root systems. Think of it as the perfect companion for rice, wheat, maize, millets, and fodder grasses!

üî¨ How Azospirillum Works: Associative Symbiosis

Associative Symbiosis means a loose partnership between bacteria and plant roots - not as intimate as Rhizobium nodules, but more specific than free-living Azotobacter.

Step 1: Chemotaxis - Finding the Roots

Azospirillum is attracted to plant roots by root exudates (organic acids, sugars, amino acids secreted by roots). The bacteria "swim" through soil water using their spiral-shaped flagella (hence "spirillum" = spiral) toward the roots.

Best attraction: Grass and cereal roots secrete specific compounds that Azospirillum loves!

Step 2: Root Colonization

Azospirillum colonizes:

  • Rhizosphere: Soil immediately surrounding roots (2-3 mm zone)
  • Rhizoplane: Root surface itself
  • Root hairs: Especially dense populations here
  • Sometimes enters root cortex (outer root tissue) but doesn't form nodules

Population: Can reach 10‚Å∑-10‚Åπ cells per gram of root!

Step 3: Nitrogen Fixation

Once established on roots, Azospirillum fixes atmospheric nitrogen (N‚ÇÇ) into ammonia (NH‚ÇÉ) using the nitrogenase enzyme. The fixed nitrogen is released into the rhizosphere where plant roots absorb it.

N‚ÇÇ (air) + Root Exudates ‚Üí NH‚ÇÉ (ammonia) ‚Üí Plant Uptake

Nitrogen contribution: 20-40 kg N/ha/year

Step 4: Plant Growth Promotion

Beyond nitrogen fixation, Azospirillum produces:

  • Auxins (IAA): Root elongation, lateral root formation (30-50% more roots!)
  • Gibberellins: Stem elongation, seed germination
  • Cytokinins: Cell division, shoot growth
  • Siderophores: Iron chelation, improved Fe uptake
  • ACC deaminase: Reduces ethylene (stress hormone), improves stress tolerance

üõ†Ô∏è Multiplication Procedure (5-7 Days)

üìÖ Day 0: Inoculation

  1. Dissolve jaggery: Mix 2 kg jaggery in 5-10 L warm water
  2. Stir until completely dissolved, then cool to room temperature (25-30¬∞C)
  3. Add Azospirillum: Pour 1 L Azospirillum culture (liquid formulation) into the jaggery solution
  4. Optional boost: Add 250-500 g besan (chickpea flour) to enhance microbial activity
  5. Fill drum: Add chlorine-free water to make 200 L total
  6. Mix thoroughly: Stir well with wooden stick for 5-10 minutes
  7. Cover loosely: Use muslin cloth (do NOT seal - bacteria need oxygen)
  8. Place in shaded location (avoid direct sunlight)

üìÜ Days 1-7: Fermentation

  • Stir twice daily: Morning and evening, 5-10 minutes each
  • Azospirillum is microaerophilic (needs some oxygen but not too much)
  • Keep in shade: Direct sunlight kills bacteria - maintain consistent temperature (25-35¬∞C)
  • Good signs:
    • Slight foam on surface
    • Sour-earthy smell (mild, not foul)
    • Cloudy appearance (bacterial growth)
    • pH drops slightly (6.0-6.5)
  • Fermentation completes: 5-7 days when smell is slightly sour-earthy and foam appears
  • Do NOT seal drum: Bacteria need air exchange

‚úÖ Ready to Use!

  • Completion indicators:
    • Consistent foamy layer on top
    • Mild sour-earthy aroma
    • Cream/white cloudy color
    • No foul odor (foul = contamination)
  • Use fresh: Best within 25-30 days of completion
  • Daily maintenance: Stir once daily during storage to maintain viability
  • Storage: Cool, shaded place (not refrigerator)
  • Filter before drip: Remove particles with cloth to prevent clogging

üí° Why "Sour-Earthy" Smell?

Azospirillum produces organic acids during growth, creating a mild sour smell. Combined with earthy compounds from bacterial metabolism, this creates the characteristic "sour-earthy" aroma. It should be pleasant and mild - not strong vinegar (like Acetobacter) or foul (contamination).

‚úÖ Best Practices & Tips

‚úÖ Do's

  • Use clean, chlorine-free water always
  • Always stir during fermentation (twice daily minimum)
  • Filter before drip/fogger to prevent clogging
  • Apply in cooler part of day (early morning/evening)
  • Use solution within 30 days of multiplication
  • Seed treatment is highly effective - prioritize this!
  • Combine with PSB and VAM for complete nutrition
  • Apply to moist soil (irrigate if dry)
  • Stir daily even during storage period
  • Store in cool, shaded environment

‚ùå Don'ts

  • Avoid chlorinated water or soap-contaminated drums
  • Don't leave undisturbed for long periods (bacteria settle)
  • Don't clog emitters with solids - always filter
  • Avoid high-heat midday applications (kills bacteria)
  • Don't store beyond a month after multiplication
  • Don't use on legumes (use Rhizobium instead)
  • Don't expose to direct sunlight during storage
  • Don't apply with fungicides/pesticides (wait 5-7 days)
  • Don't use very hot water (>40¬∞C) for multiplication
  • Don't seal drum tightly - needs air exchange

‚ö†Ô∏è Troubleshooting

Problem: Solution Settles at Bottom

After 2-3 days of storage, all bacterial mass settles at drum bottom, top water is clear.

Solution

This is normal for Azospirillum - bacteria are heavier than water and settle when not stirred. Solution: Stir vigorously for 5-10 minutes before each use to resuspend bacteria. During storage, stir once daily to keep bacteria active. Before application, mix thoroughly - you want the bacteria, not just the clear liquid!

Problem: Poor Results on Legume Crops

Applied Azospirillum to chickpea/moong/arhar but saw minimal improvement.

Solution

Azospirillum is not ideal for legumes. Legumes have specialized relationship with Rhizobium which forms root nodules and fixes 50-150 kg N/ha - far more than Azospirillum (20-40 kg). Correct approach: Use Rhizobium for legumes. Reserve Azospirillum for cereals (rice, wheat, maize), millets, grasses, sugarcane. For legumes: Rhizobium + PSB + VAM is the optimal bio-fertilizer combination.

Problem: Drip Emitters Clogging

After applying Azospirillum through drip, emitters are clogging frequently.

Solution

Causes: 1) Not filtered before application - always strain through cloth, 2) Too much bacterial mass - dilute more (15L in 250L instead of 200L), 3) Dead bacteria accumulating - use fresh culture only, 4) Jaggery residue in solution. Prevention: Double-filter through fine cloth before drip use, flush drip lines with clean water after bio-fertilizer application, use disc/screen filter at venturi, don't let solution sit more than 30 days.
