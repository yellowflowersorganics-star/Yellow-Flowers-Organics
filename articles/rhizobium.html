Rhizobium: The king of nitrogen fixation through root nodules!

ü¶† What is Rhizobium?

Rhizobium (and closely related Bradyrhizobium) is a genus of bacteria that forms the most intimate and powerful nitrogen-fixing relationship with plants. Unlike free-living Azotobacter, root-colonizing Azospirillum, or endophytic Acetobacter, Rhizobium forms root nodules - specialized structures on legume roots where atmospheric nitrogen is fixed in massive quantities!

This is true symbiosis - plant provides shelter (nodule) and food (carbohydrates), bacteria provide nitrogen (up to 150 kg/ha!). It's the perfect partnership that has evolved over millions of years. When you see pink/red nodules on legume roots, you're witnessing one of nature's most efficient nitrogen factories!

üî¨ How Rhizobium Works: The Nodulation Process

Nodulation is the formation of specialized root structures (nodules) where nitrogen fixation occurs. This is a complex, highly coordinated process:

Step 1: Recognition & Signaling

Plant signals: Legume roots secrete specific flavonoids (chemical signals) into the soil.

Bacterial response: The correct Rhizobium strain recognizes these signals and produces Nod factors (nodulation factors).

Specificity: Each legume has specific flavonoids that match with specific Rhizobium species - like a lock and key! This is why chickpea Rhizobium doesn't work well for soybean.

Step 2: Root Hair Infection

Rhizobium attaches to root hairs and causes them to curl. Bacteria enter through infection threads - tube-like structures that grow from root hair into the root cortex. Thousands of bacteria travel through these threads into the root interior.

Step 3: Nodule Formation

Plant cells in the root cortex begin dividing rapidly, forming a nodule (visible bump on roots). Bacteria are released into nodule cells where they differentiate into bacteroids - specialized nitrogen-fixing forms. The nodule develops vascular connections to the plant for nutrient exchange.

Nodule color: Pink/red color = active N-fixation (due to leghemoglobin protein, similar to blood hemoglobin!)

Step 4: Nitrogen Fixation

Inside nodules, bacteroids use the enzyme nitrogenase to convert atmospheric N‚ÇÇ into ammonia (NH‚ÇÉ). The plant provides carbohydrates (20-30% of photosynthates!) and low-oxygen environment (nitrogenase is oxygen-sensitive). Fixed nitrogen is immediately transferred to the plant.

N‚ÇÇ (air) + Plant Energy ‚Üí NH‚ÇÉ (in nodules) ‚Üí Plant Proteins

Nitrogen contribution: 50-150 kg N/ha/year depending on crop!

üõ†Ô∏è Multiplication Procedure (7-Day Cycle)

Day 0: Prepare Nutrient Medium

  1. Dissolve dry ingredients: Mix 1 kg jaggery and 1 kg gram flour in 10 L warm water
  2. Stir thoroughly until completely dissolved
  3. Pour into drum: Add to 200 L plastic drum with chlorine-free water
  4. Optional boost: Add 5-10 L cow dung slurry for added microbial support
  5. Cool solution: Ensure temperature is 25-30¬∞C before inoculation

Day 0: Add Rhizobium Starter

  1. Add liquid culture: Pour 1-2 L crop-specific Rhizobium culture into drum
  2. Or powder: Dissolve 250 g Rhizobium powder in 5 L water first, then add
  3. Mix thoroughly: Stir vigorously for 5-10 minutes
  4. Cover loosely: Use muslin cloth (bacteria need some oxygen)
  5. Place in shaded, clean, ventilated space

Days 1-7: Fermentation

  • Stir twice daily: Morning and evening, 5-10 minutes each time
  • Or use air pump: Run aquarium pump for 15-30 minutes twice daily
  • Rhizobium prefers aerobic conditions but is less oxygen-demanding than Azotobacter
  • Keep in shade: Maintain 25-30¬∞C, avoid direct sunlight
  • Good signs:
    • Light froth on surface
    • Earthy smell (not sour, not foul)
    • Cloudy appearance
    • pH stays mildly acidic to neutral (6.0-7.5)
  • Duration: 5-7 days for complete multiplication

Day 5-7: Ready to Use!

  • Completion signs:
    • Light froth and earthy smell
    • Healthy culture appearance
    • pH 6.0-7.5 (neutral range)
    • No foul odor or discoloration
  • Use immediately: Best results within 2-3 days of completion
  • Storage: Can store up to 1 week in cool, shaded place
  • Do NOT store in sunlight or sealed tanks
  • Filter before drip use: Remove particles with cloth

üí° Why Gram Flour in Multiplication?

It's ironic but true - we use nitrogen-rich gram flour (besan) to multiply nitrogen-fixing bacteria! The gram flour provides proteins, amino acids, and nutrients that Rhizobium needs to grow rapidly in the drum. Once applied to the field, the bacteria switch to fixing atmospheric nitrogen in root nodules.

Dose: 10-15 L per acre

Method: Mix with 100 kg compost/FYM and broadcast at sowing or near root zone

Timing: At sowing time or as side dressing

Note: Less efficient than seed treatment but useful for established crops

4. Drip Irrigation

Dose: 10-15 L per acre

Dilution: Mix in 200 L chlorine-free water

Filter: MUST filter through cloth to prevent emitter clogging

Timing: Early growth stage (15-20 days after sowing)

Frequency: Usually one application is sufficient

5. Foliar Spray ‚ùå

Not Recommended!

Why: Rhizobium MUST infect roots to form nodules. Cannot enter leaves. Foliar application is wasteful and ineffective

Use instead: Seed treatment or soil application

‚ö†Ô∏è Why Crop-Specific Strains Matter

Using the wrong Rhizobium strain can result in:

  • No nodulation at all (bacteria can't infect roots)
  • Poor nodulation (few, small nodules)
  • White/green nodules instead of pink/red (no active N-fixation)
  • 40-60% less nitrogen fixation
  • Wasted money and effort

Always verify: Buy crop-specific Rhizobium from trusted sources. Check label for correct species/biovar.

üîç How to Check Nodulation Success

At 30-40 days after sowing, carefully uproot 2-3 plants and check roots:

‚úÖ Successful Nodulation

  • Nodule color: Pink to red inside (leghemoglobin present)
  • Nodule size: 2-5 mm diameter (pea-sized)
  • Nodule number: 20-50+ nodules per plant (varies by crop)
  • Location: Primarily on primary and secondary roots
  • Firmness: Firm, not mushy or decayed
  • Plant appearance: Dark green leaves, vigorous growth

Action: Excellent! No nitrogen fertilizer needed. Expect good yields.

‚ö†Ô∏è Poor/Inactive Nodulation

  • Nodule color: White, green, or brown (no leghemoglobin)
  • Nodule size: Very small (<1 mm) or abnormally large
  • Nodule number: <10 nodules per plant
  • Nodule appearance: Soft, decayed, or hollow
  • Plant appearance: Pale/yellow leaves, stunted growth

Causes: Wrong Rhizobium strain, poor inoculation, acidic soil, high N fertilizer, drought stress

Action: Apply 25-50% of recommended N fertilizer. Correct issues for next season.

‚ùå No Nodulation

  • No visible nodules on any roots
  • Plant symptoms: Severe nitrogen deficiency (yellow leaves, stunted)

Causes: Wrong/incompatible Rhizobium, bacteria died (heat/chemicals), very acidic soil (pH <5.0), waterlogged conditions

Action: Apply full dose of nitrogen fertilizer immediately. Re-inoculate for next crop with correct, fresh Rhizobium.

‚ö†Ô∏è Troubleshooting

Problem: White/Green Nodules (Not Pink)

Nodules formed but they're white or green inside, not pink/red. Plants show N-deficiency.

Solution

Cause: Inactive nodulation - wrong Rhizobium strain or environmental stress. Immediate fix: Apply 50% of recommended nitrogen fertilizer (25 kg urea/acre). For next season: 1) Use correct crop-specific Rhizobium, 2) Ensure pH 6.0-7.5 (lime if acidic), 3) Provide adequate moisture, 4) Don't apply high N fertilizer at planting. Prevention: Always buy from reliable sources with crop-specific labeling.

Problem: Very Few Nodules (<10 per plant)

Nodulation occurred but number is too low for adequate N-fixation.

Solution

Causes: 1) Poor seed treatment (too little culture used), 2) Treated seeds dried in sun (killed bacteria), 3) Stored treated seeds >24 hours, 4) High nitrogen fertilizer at sowing (suppresses nodulation), 5) Acidic soil (pH <6.0), 6) Drought stress during early growth. Action: Apply supplemental nitrogen (50% dose). Next time: Use 250 mL culture per 10 kg seed, shade dry for 30 min only, sow immediately, avoid N fertilizer at sowing, irrigate regularly in first 30 days.

Problem: Nodules Formed, Then Died

Initially saw pink nodules, but later inspection shows brown, decayed nodules.

Solution

Causes: 1) Severe drought (nodules desiccate and die), 2) Waterlogging (nodules rot due to lack of oxygen), 3) High nitrogen fertilizer applied mid-season (plant stops supporting nodules), 4) Pest/disease attack on roots. Prevention: Maintain consistent soil moisture (not too wet, not too dry), avoid flooding legume fields, don't apply high N fertilizer unless nodulation fails, control root pests/diseases. Once nodules die, they rarely recover - apply rescue nitrogen fertilizer.
