Azotobacter: Nature's free nitrogen factory for all crops

üå± What is Azotobacter?

Azotobacter is a genus of free-living, nitrogen-fixing bacteria found naturally in soil. Unlike Rhizobium (which requires legumes and forms root nodules), Azotobacter works independently in the soil and benefits all types of crops - cereals, vegetables, fruits, and even legumes!

At Yellow Flowers Organic Farm, Azotobacter has been a game-changer for our non-leguminous crops like lemon, maize, and vegetables. It's like having a nitrogen fertilizer factory right in your soil - working 24/7 to capture atmospheric nitrogen and make it available to plants!

üî¨ How Azotobacter Works

Azotobacter is a free-living bacterium that lives in the soil (not inside plant roots). It uses the enzyme nitrogenase to convert atmospheric nitrogen (N‚ÇÇ) into ammonia (NH‚ÇÉ), which plants can absorb and use.

Step 1: Atmospheric Nitrogen Capture

Azotobacter absorbs nitrogen gas (N‚ÇÇ) from air pockets in the soil. The atmosphere is 78% nitrogen, but plants cannot use it directly!

Step 2: Nitrogen Fixation

Using the nitrogenase enzyme and energy from organic matter (sugars), Azotobacter converts N‚ÇÇ into ammonia (NH‚ÇÉ) and ammonium (NH‚ÇÑ‚Å∫).

N‚ÇÇ (gas) ‚Üí NH‚ÇÉ (ammonia) ‚Üí NH‚ÇÑ‚Å∫ (ammonium)

Step 3: Nitrogen Release to Soil

As Azotobacter cells die and decompose, the fixed nitrogen is released into the soil where plant roots can absorb it. Living bacteria also excrete small amounts of nitrogen compounds.

Bonus: Growth Hormones Production

Azotobacter produces plant growth hormones (auxins, gibberellins, cytokinins) that stimulate root development, shoot growth, and flowering!

üî¨ Why These Ingredients?

  • Jaggery/Molasses: Provides carbon and energy for bacterial growth

üõ†Ô∏è Multiplication Procedure (5-7 Days)

Day 0: Prepare Base Solution

  1. Boil jaggery: Dissolve 1.5-2 kg jaggery in 5-10 L water, bring to boil
  2. Boiling kills unwanted microbes and helps jaggery dissolve completely
  3. Cool completely: Let it cool to room temperature (25-30¬∞C)
  4. Add to drum: Pour into 200 L plastic drum
  5. Add chickpea flour: Mix 500 g besan into the solution, stir well to avoid lumps
  6. Fill water: Add chlorine-free water up to 200 L
  7. Well water or borewell water is best (no chlorine!)

Day 0: Inoculation

  1. Add starter culture: Pour 1-2 L Azotobacter liquid starter into the drum
  2. If using powder: Mix 1 kg powder in 5 L water first, then add
  3. Optional boost: Add 2-3 kg cow dung or compost slurry for microbial diversity
  4. Mix thoroughly: Stir vigorously for 5-10 minutes with wooden stick
  5. Cover loosely: Use muslin cloth or loose lid (don't seal tightly - needs air exchange)
  6. Place in shaded, ventilated area (not direct sunlight)

Days 1-7: Fermentation & Aeration

  • Daily stirring: Stir vigorously 2-3 times per day (morning, noon, evening)
  • Each stirring session: 5-10 minutes
  • Or use aeration pump: Run aquarium pump for 15-30 minutes, 2-3 times daily
  • Oxygen is critical - Azotobacter is aerobic!
  • Monitor fermentation: Look for:
    • Frothy surface (good sign!)
    • Mild sour/fermented smell (like buttermilk)
    • Slightly cloudy appearance
    • No foul odor (if foul smell = contamination)
  • Temperature: Optimal 25-35¬∞C (keep in shade, not too hot or cold)
  • Duration: 5-7 days for full multiplication

Day 5-7: Ready to Use!

  • Signs of readiness:
    • Consistent frothy layer on top
    • Mild fermented smell (pleasant)
    • Cloudy white/cream color
  • Quality check: pH should be 6.5-7.5 (slightly neutral)
  • Use immediately: Best results when fresh (within 2-5 days)
  • Storage: Can store in cool, shaded place for up to 1 week
  • Don't store longer - bacterial viability decreases
  • Filter before drip use: Use cloth to remove large particles

‚ö†Ô∏è Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • ‚ùå Using chlorinated water: Chlorine kills Azotobacter - use well/borewell water
  • ‚ùå Not aerating enough: Insufficient oxygen = poor multiplication - stir vigorously!
  • ‚ùå Sealing drum tightly: Bacteria need air exchange - cover loosely
  • ‚ùå Direct sunlight: UV rays kill bacteria - keep in shade always
  • ‚ùå Using hot water: Temperature >40¬∞C kills bacteria - cool solution first
  • ‚ùå Contaminated equipment: Clean all equipment thoroughly before use

1. Seed Treatment

Timing: During land preparation or at planting time

Effect: Establishes bacterial population in soil for entire season

Dose: 10-15 L per acre (multiplied culture)

Dilution: Mix in 200 L chlorine-free water

Filter: Strain through cloth to remove particles (prevent clogging)

Timing: Early morning or evening (avoid hot sun)

Frequency: Can apply 2-3 times per season (30-45 day interval)

4. Seedling Root Dip

Dilution: 1:10 (Azotobacter : water)

Method: Dip seedling roots for 30 minutes before transplanting

Best for: Vegetables (tomato, brinjal, chilli), fruit tree saplings

Effect: Faster root establishment, reduced transplant shock

5. Soil Drenching

Dose: 20-25 L multiplied culture per acre

Mix with: 100 kg compost/vermicompost

Application: Apply near root zone via flood irrigation

Timing: 15-20 days after sowing/transplanting

‚úÖ Best Practices & Tips

‚úÖ Do's

  • Always use chlorine-free water - chlorine kills bacteria
  • Apply during early morning or evening (cooler temperatures)
  • Mix with organic matter (compost/FYM) for better colonization
  • Stir/aerate vigorously during multiplication (2-3 times daily)
  • Store in cool, dark, shaded location
  • Use fresh culture (within 1 week of multiplication)
  • Apply to moist soil - bacteria need moisture to survive
  • Combine with PSB, Rhizobium, VAM for synergistic effects
  • Apply 2-3 times per season for continuous nitrogen supply
  • Check pH 6.5-7.5 during multiplication

‚ùå Don'ts

  • Don't use chlorinated tap water - use well/borewell water
  • Don't apply with chemical fungicides - wait 5-7 days
  • Don't seal drum airtight during multiplication - needs oxygen
  • Don't expose to direct sunlight - UV kills bacteria
  • Don't use with very high nitrogen fertilizers - suppresses bacterial activity
  • Don't apply to dry soil - bacteria need moisture
  • Don't store for >1 week after multiplication - viability drops
  • Don't apply during extreme heat (>40¬∞C) or cold (<10¬∞C)
  • Don't use metal containers - can react with bacterial metabolites
  • Don't skip quality check - look for froth, smell, color

‚ö†Ô∏è Troubleshooting Common Issues

Problem: Foul Smell During Multiplication

Solution smells like rotten eggs or sewage instead of mild fermented smell.

Solution

Cause: Anaerobic contamination (lack of oxygen). Fix: 1) Discard batch and start fresh, 2) Ensure vigorous daily stirring or use aeration pump, 3) Don't seal drum - allow air exchange, 4) Clean equipment thoroughly before next batch, 5) Use fresh starter culture.

Problem: No Froth or Bacterial Growth

After 7 days, solution looks clear with no froth, no fermented smell.

Solution

Causes: 1) Chlorinated water killed bacteria - use well/borewell water only, 2) Water too hot when starter added (>40¬∞C) - cool first, 3) Poor quality starter culture - get fresh culture, 4) Insufficient food - check jaggery and besan amounts, 5) Too cold (<15¬∞C) - maintain 25-35¬∞C temperature.

Problem: No Visible Results in Field

Applied Azotobacter but plants show no improvement compared to untreated.

Solution

Check: 1) Soil too dry - irrigate before/after application, 2) Very high chemical N already in soil - reduce urea for bacteria to work, 3) Recent fungicide use - wait 7 days after fungicide, 4) Application timing - apply early in season, not late, 5) Inoculum quality - use fresh, properly multiplied culture, 6) Patience - nitrogen fixation takes 3-4 weeks to show visible effects.
