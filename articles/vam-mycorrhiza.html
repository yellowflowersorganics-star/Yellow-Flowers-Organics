VAM Mycorrhiza: The Root Extension Miracle

Beneficial Fungi That Extend Roots 100x, Boost Nutrient Uptake & Drought Resistance

VAM Mycorrhiza: Nature's underground network for super-charged plant nutrition

üçÑ What is VAM (Mycorrhiza)?

Mycorrhiza (plural: mycorrhizae) means "fungus-root" - a beautiful symbiotic relationship between beneficial fungi and plant roots that has existed for 400 million years. VAM (Vesicular-Arbuscular Mycorrhiza), also called Arbuscular Mycorrhiza (AM), is the most common type, forming associations with 80-90% of all plant species!

At Yellow Flowers Organic Farm, VAM has been transformative, especially for our fruit trees and phosphorus-deficient soils. It's like giving plants a supercharged root system that reaches nutrients and water other plants can't access!

üî¨ How VAM Works: The Underground Network

Step 1: Colonization

VAM fungi penetrate plant root cells without causing harm. They form two specialized structures:

  • Arbuscules: Tree-like structures inside root cells for nutrient exchange (plant gives carbohydrates, fungus gives nutrients)
  • Vesicles: Balloon-like storage structures for nutrients and lipids

Step 2: Hyphal Network Extension

The fungus grows thread-like structures called hyphae that extend far beyond the root zone - up to 100x the root surface area! These microscopic threads (1/10th the diameter of a root hair) can:

  • Reach into tiny soil pores that roots cannot access
  • Extend 20-30 cm beyond the root zone
  • Access immobile nutrients like phosphorus

Step 3: Nutrient Exchange

The hyphal network absorbs nutrients (especially phosphorus, zinc, copper, iron) and water, transports them to arbuscules, and exchanges them with the plant for 10-20% of the plant's photosynthetic sugars. It's a fair trade - plant gets nutrients, fungus gets food!

üí° Best Host Plants for VAM Multiplication

These plants form excellent mycorrhizal associations and multiply VAM rapidly:

  • Maize (Corn): Best choice - fast growing, excellent colonization (60-80%)
  • Onion: Traditional host - very good colonization, easy to manage
  • Bajra (Pearl Millet): Drought-hardy, excellent for VAM multiplication
  • Ragi (Finger Millet): Good colonization, suitable for small-scale production
  • Sorghum (Jowar): Fast growing, high biomass, good VAM host

üõ†Ô∏è Multiplication Procedure (60 Days)

Day 0: Prepare Growing Medium

  1. Mix medium: Combine sterilized soil and sand in 1:1 ratio
  2. Alternative: Use vermiculite or cocopeat + soil (1:1)
  3. Sterilization (optional but recommended): Sun-dry medium for 3-5 days OR steam-sterilize to kill competing fungi
  4. Fill into plastic trays (depth: 15-20 cm) or raised beds (1 m¬≤ size)
  5. Leave space for watering (fill to 2-3 cm below rim)

Days 1-7: Plant & Inoculate

  1. Sow host plants: Plant maize/onion/bajra seeds OR transplant 15-day-old seedlings
  2. Spacing: 30-40 plants per m¬≤ (don't overcrowd)
  3. Inoculate with VAM: Sprinkle 100-200g VAM starter per m¬≤ or per tray
  4. Mix inoculum into top 5-7 cm of medium around root zones
  5. Water gently with rose can (avoid washing away VAM)
  6. Place under 50-70% shade net

Days 8-60: Growth & Colonization

  • Water daily: Keep medium moist (not waterlogged)
  • Early morning watering is best
  • No fertilizers: Do NOT add any fertilizers - limits VAM colonization
  • Avoid phosphorus especially - suppresses VAM development
  • Keep under partial sun or shade (prevents heat stress)
  • Weeks 4-8: Plants may look slightly stressed (pale green) - this is good! Nutrient stress promotes VAM colonization
  • Allow plants to grow for 45-60 days minimum

Signs of Good Colonization: Root mass is dense, white mycelia visible on roots, plants look healthy despite no fertilizer

Day 60: Harvest Inoculum

  1. Stop watering 2-3 days before harvest (easier to handle)
  2. Uproot plants: Carefully remove entire root system with soil
  3. Cut roots: Chop roots into small pieces (0.5-1 cm length)
  4. Mix thoroughly: Combine chopped roots + soil + sand mixture
  5. Spread in shade to semi-dry (not fully dry - keep some moisture)
  6. Store: In breathable bags (jute/cloth) in cool, dry place
  7. Yield: 1-2 kg starter ‚Üí 50-100 kg VAM inoculum!

üßë‚Äçüåæ Application Methods

1. Seed Treatment

Rate: 100g VAM per kg seeds

Method: Mix VAM with seeds using sticker (rice gruel, jaggery solution, gum arabic)

Drying: Shade dry for 30 minutes, sow immediately

Best for: Cereals, pulses, vegetables

2. Root Dipping (Transplants)

Preparation: Mix 500g VAM with 10-15L water + cow dung slurry

Method: Dip seedling roots for 15-30 minutes before transplanting

Benefits: 40-60% reduced transplant shock, faster establishment

Best for: Vegetables, fruit tree saplings

3. Soil Application - Trees

Dose: 100-200g per tree near root zone

Method: Make 3-4 holes (15cm deep) around tree, add VAM, cover

Timing: During planting or rainy season for established trees

Effect: Long-lasting (3-5 years for perennials)

4. Soil Application - Field Crops

Dose: 5-10 kg per acre

Method: Mix with FYM/compost, apply in root zone during planting

Application: Band placement near seed rows or transplant holes

Works for: Vegetables, cereals, pulses

5. Pot Plants / Nursery

Small pots: 25-50g per pot

Large pots: 100-200g per pot

Method: Mix into potting medium or apply around root area

Timing: During repotting or planting

‚ö†Ô∏è Special Considerations

üö´ High-Phosphorus Soils

If soil P is already very high (>40 ppm), VAM effectiveness reduces. Plants don't "need" the fungus when P is abundant, so colonization is suppressed. Test soil before investing in VAM.

üíä Chemical Fungicides

Fungicides like carbendazim, mancozeb kill mycorrhizal fungi. Avoid fungicide use 15 days before and after VAM application. Trichoderma and VAM can coexist - they're compatible!

üå± Works Best with Organic Matter

VAM colonization is 2-3x better in soils with good organic matter (2-3%). Always apply VAM mixed with compost or FYM for best results.

‚è∞ Long-Term Investment

VAM doesn't show instant results like chemical fertilizers. Benefits appear after 30-45 days once colonization is established. For perennials (fruit trees), effects last 3-5 years - one-time application!

üå°Ô∏è Temperature Sensitivity

VAM works best at 20-35¬∞C. Very high temperatures (>45¬∞C) or cold (<10¬∞C) reduce activity. Store inoculum in cool, dry place (not refrigerator - maintains room temperature).

‚úÖ Best Practices & Tips

‚úÖ Do's

  • Apply at planting/transplanting - early colonization is most effective
  • Mix with organic matter (compost/FYM) - boosts colonization
  • Apply in root zone - fungus needs contact with roots
  • Store in cool, dry, shaded place away from sunlight
  • Check crop compatibility before application
  • Use low-P fertilizers - high P suppresses VAM
  • Avoid fungicides for 15 days before and after
  • Maintain soil moisture - VAM needs water to function
  • Apply annually for annuals, once for perennials
  • Combine with Rhizobium for legumes - synergistic effect

‚ùå Don'ts

  • Don't use on Brassicaceae (cabbage family) - waste of money
  • Don't apply to waterlogged soils - VAM needs oxygen
  • Don't use with chemical fungicides - kills VAM
  • Don't apply high phosphorus fertilizers with VAM
  • Don't expect instant results - takes 30-45 days
  • Don't store in direct sunlight or heat
  • Don't apply to very acidic soils (pH <5.0) - adjust pH first
  • Don't use expired inoculum (shelf life: 6-12 months)
  • Don't skip soil testing - know your P levels
  • Don't apply surface only - incorporate into root zone

‚ö†Ô∏è Troubleshooting Common Issues

Problem: No Visible Improvement

Applied VAM but plants show no difference compared to untreated.

Solution

Check: 1) Crop compatibility - is it mycorrhizal?, 2) Soil P - if very high (>40 ppm), VAM won't colonize, 3) Time - wait 45-60 days for colonization, 4) Fungicide use - did you apply fungicides?, 5) Application method - was VAM in root zone?, 6) Inoculum quality - check expiry date, storage conditions.

Problem: Poor Multiplication Results

Host plants died or very little inoculum produced after 60 days.

Solution

Common mistakes: 1) Added fertilizers - starve plants for better colonization!, 2) Medium not sterile - competing fungi reduced VAM, 3) Overwatering - waterlogged conditions kill VAM, 4) Too much sun - keep under 50-70% shade, 5) Harvested too early - wait full 60 days minimum, 6) Wrong host plant - use maize/onion for best results.

Problem: Stored VAM Lost Viability

VAM inoculum stored for 3-6 months shows no colonization when used.

Solution

Storage issues: 1) Too hot - store at room temp (20-30¬∞C), not in heat, 2) Too wet - keep dry but not bone-dry (10-15% moisture), 3) Sealed tightly - use breathable bags (jute/cloth), 4) Direct sunlight - UV kills spores, store in dark, 5) Metal containers - use plastic/cloth bags only. Solution: Use within 6-12 months, store properly, check viability before large-scale use.
